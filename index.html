Hi! I'm Andrew.

I'm an emergency physician and researcher interested in the moments where medicine becomes urgent, uncertain, and very human: resuscitation, cardiovascular emergencies, and the systems that shape population health.

My work explores how data and artificial intelligence can help clinicians make better decisions when time is short and the stakes are high. I'm especially interested in tools that are fair, trustworthy, and useful in the real world.

This site is a small home for the questions I keep returning to, the projects I'm building, and the ideas I'm still thinking through. LinkedIn can keep the CV. This is where I try to sound a little more like a person.

Who is Andrew?

Outside work, family life is where I am most reliably reminded that not everything important can be measured, predicted, triaged, or optimized.

It keeps me grounded in ordinary things: meals, routines, small jokes, minor chaos, and the humbling discovery that clinical decision-making is sometimes easier than getting everyone out of the house on time.

That part of life quietly shapes how I think about medicine and technology. The best tools should not make us less attentive to people. They should help us notice more, care better, and make room for what matters.

Work

Questions I keep returning to

How can we spot trouble earlier?

In emergencies, a few minutes can change everything. The earlier we recognise that someone is seriously unwell, the earlier the right help can begin.

I'm interested in how we can spot danger sooner - whether that means recognising cardiac arrest before the ambulance arrives, noticing subtle signs that someone is deteriorating, or using technology to alert the right people at the right time.

Put simply: how do we help people get the care they need before it becomes too late?

How can AI help without making healthcare less human?

Artificial intelligence is often talked about as if it is here to replace people. I'm more interested in how it can help people do their best work.

In emergency medicine, decisions are often made quickly, with incomplete information, and under pressure. AI may help by noticing patterns, reducing blind spots, and supporting clinicians when things are busy or uncertain.

But it also needs guardrails. It should be fair. It should be explainable. It should not quietly make care worse for certain groups of people. Most importantly, it should help doctors and nurses pay more attention to patients - not less.

The goal is not colder, faster medicine. The goal is better care.

What are we missing when someone comes in with chest pain, fainting, or palpitations?

Many people come to the emergency department with symptoms that are worrying but unclear. Chest pain. Fainting. A racing heartbeat. Breathlessness. A strange feeling that something is not quite right.

Most of the time, these symptoms are not due to something life-threatening. But sometimes they are.

One of the hardest parts of emergency medicine is working out who needs urgent treatment, who needs more tests, and who can safely go home. I'm interested in how we can make those decisions more accurately, more fairly, and with less guesswork.

Because sometimes the most important diagnosis is the one that is easy to miss.
